French President Emmanuel Macron and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz clashed with European Council President António Costa during a closed-door Brussels summit on June 19, 2026. The dispute erupted after Costa attempted to establish secret diplomatic communication channels with the Kremlin via a senior aide. Macron and Merz blocked the outreach, arguing that major powers, rather than EU institutions, should lead negotiations with Russia.

The dispute centers on a conflict between two diplomatic models. António Costa sought to establish "institutional diplomacy" by positioning the EU as a direct, sovereign actor. His chief of staff, Pedro Lourtie, made two phone calls to a senior Russian official close to Vladimir Putin in recent weeks to open these channels.

Macron and Merz advocated for a "power diplomacy" model. During the summit, they argued that current timing for negotiations is inappropriate and that "major powers"—specifically France, Germany, and the UK—should lead any future diplomatic engagement. By including the UK, a non-EU member, the Franco-German axis reinforced the logic that geopolitical weight outweighs institutional mandate.

The reaction from Paris and Berlin suggests they perceived the move as an attempt to bypass the traditional leadership roles of the EU's two largest economies. Costa's office and an EU official stated that "nothing was discussed on substance" and the calls were technical. An EU official defended the outreach, stating the EU has "specific interests that will need to be defended" and requires established channels, though the EU does not intend to act as a mediator.

Reactions across the bloc were divided. Estonia, Denmark, and the Netherlands aligned with Macron and Merz. Other EU leaders defended Costa's efforts, arguing that communication channels serve the Union's broader strategic interests. Belgian Prime Minister Bart De Wever noted that future negotiations will require a formal determination of who represents the bloc if Russia shows a willingness to engage.

The clash between the European Council and the Franco-German axis underscores a tension over the EU's diplomatic agency. While the European Council maintains that established channels are necessary to defend the Union's interests, Paris and Berlin prefer a leadership model centered on geopolitical weight. The bloc remains divided on the legitimacy of institutional outreach, leaving the question of who represents the European Union in future negotiations with Russia as a point of structural contention.
