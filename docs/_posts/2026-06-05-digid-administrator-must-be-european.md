The Dutch government blocked the acquisition of Solvinity, the IT operator of the national digital identity system DigiD, by US-based Kyndryl in late May 2026. Officials cited national security and digital sovereignty concerns, specifically the risk that US authorities could access critical infrastructure data via the US CLOUD Act, bypassing European legal protections.

The government executed the block under the Netherlands' sector-specific telecommunications investment screening regime. It cited "public interest concerns," focusing on the risk that critical national infrastructure could fall under non-EU legal frameworks. Officials specifically identified the US CLOUD Act, which allows American authorities to demand data access from US-based companies regardless of where the data is stored.

The Dutch Authority for Consumers and Markets (ACM) had cleared the approximately €100 million acquisition on competition grounds in February 2026. However, the national security review overturned the market-based approval, prioritizing digital sovereignty over corporate acquisition.

Kyndryl expressed disappointment, stating that "the politicization of this process has overshadowed the clear and important benefits this transaction would have brought."

The decision follows months of pressure from the Dutch House of Representatives. Members of parliament urged the government to reconsider contracts with Solvinity if the provider could not guarantee that the national digital identity system remained shielded from extraterritorial legal reach.

This move aligns with the broader European effort to implement the European Digital Identity (eID) Wallet. The framework, overseen by the European Commission, aims to provide all EU citizens with a secure and interoperable digital identity by the end of 2026, reducing reliance on non-European technology providers for critical identity infrastructure.
