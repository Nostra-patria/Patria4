Russian oil giant Lukoil and the Kinahan organized crime group used Irish-registered subsidiaries to bypass EU sanctions. Ireland's restrictive corporate transparency laws created a systemic vulnerability in the European Single Market, allowing sanctioned actors to funnel oil and capital through Northern Ireland and other Irish entities to evade international restrictions.

The evasion strategy relies on the "freedom of establishment" principle, which allows companies to register in any EU member state. By creating subsidiaries in Northern Ireland, Lukoil and other sanctioned entities operate under the legal cover of the Single Market. The Kinahan group integrated Russian state-owned banks into laundering networks to move capital and oil across borders.

Ireland's corporate registry historically functioned as a "registry shield" due to restrictive access policies. Until recently, the state often required evidence of criminal proceedings before granting access to beneficial ownership information. This created a circular barrier: investigators needed registry data to prove a crime, but required a proven crime to access the data.

The European Commission is intervening to close these gaps. The Commission is mandating that all EU member states adopt common standards for "legitimate interest" by July 2026 to ensure journalists and civil society can access corporate registries. This follows a CJEU ruling that limited public access to beneficial ownership registers, leaving member states to define who qualifies as having a legitimate interest.

To combat these vulnerabilities, the EU adopted its 20th Sanctions Package on April 23, 2026. This package activates an "anti-circumvention tool" to restrict exports to third countries that facilitate sanctions evasion. The tool shifts the EU's focus from targeting individual entities to disrupting the trade flows used by "hub" countries to shield Russian assets.

EU member states must adopt common "legitimate interest" standards for corporate registry access by July 2026. This deadline follows efforts to close "registry shield" vulnerabilities that allowed sanctioned actors to exploit national secrecy laws. These measures complement the anti-circumvention tools in the 20th Sanctions Package to disrupt the trade flows and corporate structures used to bypass restrictions on Russia.
