In the plenary of the European Parliament, the rhythmic chants of "send them back" from right-wing MEPs are often dismissed as political theater. They are, in reality, the audible manifestation of a structural vulnerability being weaponized. From the Russian-funded networks that fragmented Moldova's 2025 elections to the "shadow alliance" between Kremlin agents and organized crime, Moscow is no longer merely seeking to persuade European audiences. It is industrializing the exploitation of the tension between national sovereignty and EU federation, treating the bloc's democratic openness as a delivery system for decision-making gridlock.

## The Doctrine of Systemic Decay

Modern Russian influence operations are not exercises in persuasion, but applications of a doctrinal evolution from Soviet-era "reflexive control" and *maskirovka*. While traditional propaganda seeks to change what an adversary thinks, reflexive control aims to change how an adversary decides. The objective is to feed the target specific information that compels them to make a decision that serves the Kremlin's interests, often while the target believes they are acting autonomously.

In the current European context, this has evolved into a strategy of systemic decay. Moscow's approach mirrors a cyber-attack on the "CIA triad"—Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability—but applied to the cognitive and political processes of decision-making. By compromising the integrity of shared facts and the availability of a coherent political center, Russia degrades the EU's capacity to formulate and execute a unified response to crises. The goal is not necessarily the victory of a specific ideology, but the induction of a state of permanent institutional paralysis where the cost of decision-making becomes prohibitively high.

This doctrinal shift suggests that the Kremlin—and the various competing intelligence factions within the Russian state—sees the European political system not as a set of opponents to be defeated, but as a machine to be jammed. By introducing noise and contradiction into the decision-making loop, the Russian state ensures that the EU spends more energy managing internal friction than projecting external power. While these operations are often opportunistic and characterized by internal factionalism between agencies like the GRU and SVR, the emergent pattern is a transition from the "Active Measures" of the Cold War to a model of systemic warfare. The target is the process of governance itself.

## The Sovereignty Gap: Amplification vs. Invention

The primary structural vulnerability being weaponized is the inherent tension between national sovereignty and European federation. It is critical to recognize that Russia does not create these fissures; it identifies existing political anxieties regarding the loss of national autonomy and amplifies them to fragment pro-EU unity. The "sovereignty gap"—the friction between the desire for local control and the necessity of federal scale—is a domestic European reality with deep historical roots.

The nationalist pushback against migration, the frustration over perceived EU overreach, and the grievances stemming from the 2015 migrant crisis are organic. These sentiments are not Russian inventions; they are the result of democratic deficits and policy failures within the bloc. However, the Kremlin's strategy is to treat these genuine grievances as raw material. By amplifying the most extreme poles of these debates, Moscow ensures that organic political dissent is transformed into a tool for systemic disruption.

The operationalization of this strategy was evident in Moldova's 2025 elections. Secret Russian-funded networks were deployed to amplify pro-Russian narratives, specifically targeting the friction between the pro-EU ruling party and nationalist factions. By framing EU integration as an erasure of national identity, Moscow fragmented the political landscape. This demonstrated that while the grievances are real, the scaling and direction of the anger are externally managed. When the "send them back" chants echo in the European Parliament, they signal a similar process: the transformation of a legitimate political debate over borders into a weapon for institutional paralysis.

## The Industrialization of Influence

The transition from state-led propaganda to a hybrid, "grey" infrastructure has allowed the Kremlin to scale its impact while masking its hand. This is no longer a matter of a few diplomats or state-run media outlets, but an industrialized pipeline of influence. Central to this is the "Pravda Dashboard," an automated content-repurposing system that allows the Kremlin to reuse narratives across multiple regions and languages. This ensures that a single structural vulnerability can be exploited simultaneously across different member states, though the execution varies in competence and quality across different regional hubs.

This digital industrialization is supported by a "shadow alliance" between Russian state actors and organized criminal networks. By partnering with gangs specialized in migrant smuggling and cybercrime, the Kremlin bridges the gap between digital noise and physical disruption. This alliance provides the state with a layer of plausible deniability; the actors on the ground are mercenaries and criminals, not soldiers or intelligence officers. This shift to a decentralized, proxy-led model ensures that while the origin of the operation remains obscured, the resulting systemic stress on European security and social cohesion is magnified.

The use of federal agencies like Rossotrudnichestvo alongside these proxy botnets creates a tiered system of influence. Official channels provide a veneer of diplomatic legitimacy; automated systems like the Pravda Dashboard ensure narrative saturation; criminal proxies execute the physical manifestations of the campaign. This layered approach makes the network resilient to conventional counter-intelligence. Removing a single botnet or expelling a few diplomats does not disrupt the underlying industrial pipeline, as the network is designed to be redundant and opportunistic.

## From Digital Noise to Physical Sabotage

The "shadow alliance" between the Kremlin and organized crime creates a direct pipeline from digital coordination to physical destabilization. This transition is fueled by an "anti-everything" signal propagated across social networks, designed to cultivate societal decay. By amplifying extreme poles of existing vulnerabilities, Russian networks identify and recruit individuals with criminal backgrounds who are already alienated from the state.

These recruited proxies are then deployed to carry out acts of physical sabotage, targeting critical infrastructure such as energy grids and undersea cables. The strategic logic is not the total destruction of the target, but the creation of systemic anxiety. By bridging the gap between a digital campaign and a physical arson attack or cable cut, Moscow demonstrates that the "grey zone" is a staging ground for kinetic operations. The socio-economic vulnerability of these criminal actors is weaponized, turning the marginalized fringes of European society into a decentralized army of sabotage.

This process transforms the "anti-everything" sentiment from a digital mood into a physical threat. When a criminal proxy is paid to damage a power pylon, the act is not always motivated by a coherent political ideology, but by a combination of financial incentive and a general hatred of the system. This makes the sabotage difficult to deter through traditional means. The actors have no political stakes in the outcome and no central command structure that can be threatened with sanctions, making the operation a series of disconnected tactical wins that aggregate into a strategic burden.

## The Accountability Gap and Systemic Stress

The decentralized nature of these physical attacks creates an accountability gap that tests the limits of European resilience. According to the IISS database, Russian sabotage efforts against critical infrastructure have remained largely unaffected by the conventional responses of NATO and the EU. Because the attacks are carried out by criminal proxies rather than uniformed state actors, the traditional mechanisms of deterrence—diplomatic sanctions or military posturing—are ineffective.

This lack of a clear adversary to hold accountable induces systemic stress. When a power grid fails or a communication cable is severed by an unattributable actor, the resulting vacuum of responsibility erodes public trust in the state's primary function: the provision of security. The "grey zone" escalation, as warned by the AIVD and MIVD, is designed to make the state appear impotent in the face of invisible threats. The structural consequence is a gradual degradation of the social contract. The citizen no longer perceives the state as a guarantor of stability, but as a bystander to an escalating, unattributable conflict.

This creates a classic hybrid dynamic where the state's response itself becomes ammunition—framed as overreach—to further erode trust. As the state increases surveillance and policing to catch these invisible actors, it may inadvertently fuel the "anti-everything" sentiment that the Kremlin is amplifying. The state's attempt to close the gap is then framed by Russian networks as an assault on civil liberties, further widening the divide between the citizen and the institution. The response to the attack becomes the next piece of evidence used by the adversary to prove the state's dysfunction.

## Conclusion: The Hollowing of the Center

The objective of these combined efforts—from reflexive control and the exploitation of sovereignty fissures to the industrialization of sabotage—is the systematic destruction of the political center. By treating the tension between national sovereignty and EU federation not as a problem to be solved, but as a vulnerability to be widened, the Kremlin seeks to render the European project incapable of governance.

This is the final stage of the "systemic warfare" model. When the political center is hollowed out, replaced by polarized extremes and a populace distrustful of its own institutions, the capacity for collective decision-making vanishes. The EU does not need to be defeated in a conventional conflict; it only needs to be induced into a state of permanent institutional paralysis. In this environment, the "Sovereignty Fissure" becomes a permanent chasm, leaving a fragmented Europe unable to project power or maintain internal cohesion. 

The evidence indicates that Russia has evolved its traditional "Active Measures" into a digitally-industrialized model of systemic warfare. By moving beyond simple persuasion to target the structural vulnerabilities of European liberal democracies—specifically the tension between national sovereignty and EU federation—the Kremlin is not seeking to win an ideological argument, but to induce institutional paralysis. By bridging the gap between digital noise and physical sabotage via criminal proxies, Moscow seeks to hollow out the political center and degrade the bloc's capacity for collective decision-making.

The structural reality is that the EU's democratic openness is being used as a vector for its own decay. Unless the bloc can move beyond reactive sanctions and address the underlying "soignty gap" that Moscow exploits, the institutional paralysis will likely transition from a tactical goal to a permanent condition. The fight is no longer about the truth of a specific narrative, but about the survival of the capacity to decide.
